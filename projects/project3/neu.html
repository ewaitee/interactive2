New Alphabet

Designed by Wim Crouwel in 1967

About New Alphabet


In the infancy of digital typographyâ€”as lead type, set by hand in heavy lead blocks or by machines that generated lines of metal type, was giving way to text set on screens Crouwel saw an opportunity for an interesting experiment. Early computer screenscathode ray tube CRT monitors rendered images in fairly large pixels, making traditional curvilinear letterforms difficult to reconstruct, and so Crouwel set out to redesign the alphabet using only horizontal lines. New Alphabet is, in Crouwel's words, "over-the-top and never meant to be really used," a statement on the impact of new technologies on centuries of typographic tradition. In 1988, however, Peter Saville Associates used a stylized version of the font on the cover of Substance, an album for the band Joy Division. New Alphabet was digitized for contemporary use in 1997 by Freda Sack and David Quay of The Foundry, closely based on Crouwel's original studies.
